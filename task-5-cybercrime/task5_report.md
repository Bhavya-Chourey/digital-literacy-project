## Part A – Case Study Summary

**Cybercrime Type:** UPI / Online Payment Fraud
**Scenario:** Realistic Fictional Scenario

UPI fraud is a form of digital financial crime where attackers deceive victims into authorizing fraudulent transactions through psychological manipulation. It is one of the fastest-growing cybercrime categories in India due to the widespread adoption of apps like Google Pay, PhonePe, and Paytm.

The case study follows a first-year college student in Madhya Pradesh who lost ₹8,500 — money saved for semester books — after scanning a fraudulent QR code sent by a fake buyer for a second-hand laptop listed on an online marketplace.

**How it typically happens:**
1. Attacker identifies victim selling something online
2. Poses as a genuine buyer (often impersonating army/government identity)
3. Sends a **collect request** QR code claiming it will *receive* money
4. Victim enters UPI PIN → money is **debited**, not credited
5. Funds are routed through mule accounts and the fraudster vanishes

## Part B – Prevention Checklist Summary

> Full 10-point checklist in `task-5-cybercrime/prevention-checklist.md`

Key tips include:
- Never share OTP, UPI PIN, CVV, or passwords with anyone
- Understand that you **never** need to enter your UPI PIN to *receive* money
- Set daily UPI transaction limits and enable SMS alerts
- Avoid scanning unknown QR codes from unverified individuals
- Report immediately to **1930** or [cybercrime.gov.in](https://cybercrime.gov.in)

## Reflection

What surprised me most while researching UPI fraud was discovering that the core deception exploits a fundamental misunderstanding about how UPI works: most people do not know that entering your UPI PIN on a collect request sends money **out** of your account, not into it. Fraudsters deliberately exploit this knowledge gap. I also did not realize how quickly funds get laundered through chains of mule bank accounts, making recovery nearly impossible even if the fraud is reported within minutes.

I was also struck by how convincing the social engineering tactics are — fraudsters often pose as army officers or government employees, creating a sense of authority and urgency that overrides the victim's natural caution. This is not about technical sophistication; it is entirely psychological.

**One habit I will personally change:** I will never scan an unknown QR code or click a payment link from someone I have not verified in person — regardless of how credible their story sounds. I will also enable daily UPI transaction limits and ensure SMS alerts are active on all my accounts.
