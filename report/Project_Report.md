## Introduction

This project report documents my work as a Student Digital Ambassador for CSE0001 – Digital Literacy at VIT Bhopal University. The assignment required me to build a comprehensive Digital Literacy Portfolio spread across five distinct tasks, each aligned with a module of the course.

The portfolio covers a wide range of digital competencies: creating an awareness infographic, building a professional online presence, exploring coding and collaboration platforms, practising professional email communication, and researching cybercrime awareness. The overarching goal of this project is to develop practical digital skills that are directly applicable in academic, professional, and everyday life — and to share those skills with peers in my role as a Digital Ambassador.

## Task 1 – Digital Literacy Awareness Infographic

### Tool Used

For this task, I used Canva (canva.com), a free browser-based design platform that requires no prior graphic design experience. Canva offers a wide library of templates, icons, and color schemes that made it straightforward to create a visually engaging, single-page infographic.

### What the Design Shows

My infographic is titled **'Be a Smart Digital Citizen'** and is designed to be shared with first-year batchmates who may be unfamiliar with the fundamentals of digital literacy. The infographic covers three core topics:

- **What Digital Literacy Is** – A clear, student-friendly definition emphasizing that digital literacy goes beyond knowing how to use devices; it includes evaluating information critically, communicating responsibly, and staying safe online.

- **Safe Internet Practices** – Key habits such as using strong passwords, enabling two-factor authentication, recognizing phishing links, and avoiding oversharing personal information on social media.

- **Professional Online Presence** – A brief guide to building a credible digital footprint through platforms like GitHub and LinkedIn, and the importance of keeping profiles clean and up to date.

### Reflection

The most interesting part of making this infographic was the challenge of condensing complex ideas into short, visual-friendly text without losing meaning. I initially tried to include too much information and had to cut it down significantly to keep the design readable. What I found difficult was choosing a consistent color palette that felt both professional and approachable for a student audience. Ultimately, I settled on a blue-and-white scheme that felt trustworthy and clean. This task made me appreciate how much thought goes into good visual communication.

## Task 4 – Professional Email & Etiquette Guide

### Email Drafts

I wrote two professional emails as part of this task. The first was from a student to a professor requesting an assignment deadline extension, citing a genuine academic reason and maintaining a respectful, formal tone throughout. The second was a cold outreach email to an internship coordinator, expressing interest in a summer internship opportunity in AI/ML and briefly highlighting relevant skills and motivation.

Both emails follow professional email conventions: a clear subject line, a formal salutation, a well-structured body with a single focused purpose, and a courteous sign-off. Writing these emails made me realize how much tone and word choice matter — the same request can come across as entitled or respectful depending entirely on how it is phrased.

### Communication Scenario – When Poor Digital Communication Causes Problems

A common scenario that illustrates the consequences of poor digital communication is when a student sends a casual, poorly worded message to a professor — for example: *'hey sir, can i get more time for the assignment lol, been busy.'* While the student may have had a legitimate reason for the request, the informal tone, lack of subject context, and use of slang immediately undermine credibility. The professor, receiving dozens of emails daily, may dismiss this without response or form a negative impression of the student.

**What could have been done differently:** The student should have used a proper subject line ('Request for Assignment Deadline Extension – [Course Name]'), addressed the professor formally ('Dear Professor [Name]'), briefly explained the genuine reason, and closed professionally. A single well-written email takes two minutes more but leaves a lasting positive impression — and is far more likely to receive a sympathetic response.

## Task 5 – Cybercrime Awareness Case Study & Prevention Guide

### Cybercrime Chosen: UPI / Online Payment Fraud

I chose UPI/Online Payment Fraud as my case study topic because it is the most prevalent and rapidly growing form of cybercrime in India, particularly among students and young adults who are active digital payment users. The case study describes a realistic fictional scenario of a first-year college student who loses money after scanning a fraudulent QR code sent by a fake buyer on a second-hand marketplace.

Researching this topic revealed the full step-by-step mechanism of UPI fraud — from target selection and impersonation, to the deceptive use of collect requests and QR codes that debit rather than credit funds. The case study covers who is typically targeted, how the crime unfolds, and the financial and psychological consequences.

### What Surprised Me Most

What surprised me most while researching UPI fraud was discovering that the core deception exploits a fundamental misunderstanding about how UPI works: most people do not know that entering your UPI PIN on a collect request sends money **out** of your account, not into it. Fraudsters deliberately exploit this knowledge gap. I also did not realize how quickly funds get laundered through chains of 'mule' bank accounts, making recovery nearly impossible even if the fraud is reported within minutes.

I was also surprised by how convincing the social engineering tactics are — fraudsters often pose as army officers or government employees, which creates a sense of authority and urgency that overrides the victim's natural caution. This is not about technical sophistication; it is entirely psychological.

### One Habit I Will Personally Change

As a direct result of this research, I will never scan an unknown QR code or click a payment link from someone I have not verified in person — regardless of how credible their story sounds. I will also immediately enable daily UPI transaction limits and ensure that SMS transaction alerts are active on all my accounts so I can detect unauthorized activity the moment it occurs.
